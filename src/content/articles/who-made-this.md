You read something that stops you cold. A paragraph, a melody, an image. Before you ask what it means, before you decide whether you agree, a more basic question arrives: *who made this?*

Increasingly, there is no answer. The text in front of you might be someone's life's work, or the statistical residue of a million people's life's work, and nothing in the text itself will tell you which. The machines got fluent. The flood is here, and everything in it is polished, plausible, and authorless.

I don't think the craving underneath that question is new. We never wanted just the words. We wanted the person behind the words — to know that someone meant this, that it cost them something. 

## The opposite of its promise

Copyright was not invented for authors. It was invented for printers. In 1557, Queen Mary I granted the [Stationers' Company](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Worshipful_Company_of_Stationers_and_Newspaper_Makers) — a guild of London publishers — a monopoly on printing, in exchange for help suppressing seditious and heretical books. It was a censorship arrangement dressed as a trade privilege, and the "copy right" it created belonged to the publisher, not the writer, in perpetuity.

The [Statute of Anne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statute_of_Anne) in 1710 broke that perpetual monopoly — on paper. The publishers spent the next sixty-four years claiming copyright was still perpetual at common law, until the House of Lords settled it in [*Donaldson v Beckett*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donaldson_v_Beckett) in 1774. The first copyright law was a *reduction* of rights. Fourteen years, renewable once, after which the work belonged to everyone. Authors were placed at the center of the story, but the deal was utilitarian from the start: a short, artificial scarcity, granted because printing a book was expensive and copying it was cheap.

Copyright took a specific technological accident — that reproduction was a discrete, controllable, expensive act — and built a property right on top of it. Jefferson [saw the tension early](https://founders.archives.gov/documents/Jefferson/03-06-02-0207): "He who receives an idea from me, receives instruction himself without lessening mine; as he who lights his taper at mine, receives light without darkening me." Inventions, he concluded, "cannot, in nature, be a subject of property." The law worked anyway. It glued together two things with no natural connection: the right to stop copies, and the link between a work and its maker. For three centuries they traveled as one. We forgot they were ever separate.

The promise is written into the law itself. "The encouragement of learning," the statute called it. "To promote the progress of science and useful arts," says the US constitution. The theory: grant creators a temporary monopoly and they will create more. Everyone wins.

The record is something else. Culture was always a remix. Shakespeare lifted his plots from chronicles and earlier plays without a flicker of shame. Bach reworked his own themes and everyone else's. Folk music is a centuries-long chain of copying with variation. Nobody creates from nothing; creativity is absorption and recombination. The law then spent three hundred years criminalizing exactly that metabolism.

The fourteen-year term is now life plus seventy years, extended again and again — most famously by a [company](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copyright_Term_Extension_Act) that built its empire animating public-domain fairy tales and then lobbied to lock the door behind it. Millions of works became [orphans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orphan_work): still under copyright, commercially worthless, illegal to touch, their owners untraceable. Culturally dead.

Hip-hop began as a living conversation with musical history — DJs and producers cutting the past into the present. It got killed by lawsuits: [*Grand Upright*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grand_Upright_Music,_Ltd._v._Warner_Bros._Records_Inc.) in 1991, where the judge opened his opinion with "Thou shalt not steal," and [*Bridgeport*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bridgeport_Music,_Inc._v._Dimension_Films) in 2005, where the Sixth Circuit ruled "get a license or do not sample." The collage aesthetic became a luxury good.

Wherever the fence doesn't reach, creativity explodes. Memes, fan fiction, open source software, early hip-hop itself. The law that claimed to feed creativity ended up starving it.

## The machine is a reader

Now the machines arrive, and the first instinct is to call them thieves. Look at what they actually do. A language model reads. It absorbs facts, structures, styles — the statistical shape of expression — and produces new work from what it learned. Facts were never copyrightable. Style was never copyrightable. A human who spends years absorbing a genre and then writes in it owes nobody anything; the machine does the same thing at scale. I've [argued this at length before](/articles/the-tool-is-not-the-author/).

The "is training stealing?" debate assumes the law ever policed learning. It didn't. It policed *copies* — the one act it could see, count, and forbid — and let the copy stand in for everything it couldn't reach: the reading, the influence, the inspiration.

A language model learns *by* copying. Reading and copying are now the same act, performed at a scale no licensing regime can process. The stand-in no longer stands in: to control copies now is to control computation itself. No court can restore that distinction. Copyright won't be repealed. It will be bypassed.

## Who would miss it?

Run the thought experiment. Copyright disappears tomorrow. Who suffers?

Not the biggest players — they would be fine either way. They thrived under maximal copyright, and in a world of free copies they still hold the platforms, the distribution and the budgets. Amazon doesn't need copyright to sell you a Dickens novel.

Some real people do get hurt, and it's worth saying who. The mid-list author living off backlist royalties. The freelance photographer whose business *is* licensing. The specialist who spent three years on a field guide. These are not moguls; they're working freelancers, and for them the exclusive right to copies is the income. Abolition without a replacement would push some of them out of creative work entirely. That's a real cost.

But look at everyone else. Musicians earn from performance, sessions, teaching, merchandise — streaming already pays most of them almost nothing, and they've adapted. Visual artists sell objects and commissions. Filmmakers run on grants and crowdfunding. Academics give their work away and watch publishers collect the tolls. Journalists are paid for the work, not the copy. The vast majority of people who create never lived off copies in the first place. The protection was always thinner than the rhetoric.

## People pay the person

The economist's objection writes itself: if perfect copies are free and legal, rational consumers pay nothing, and creators starve. It's a clean theory with one flaw — its human is fictional. Homo economicus, the creature who acts only on price, doesn't exist. The evidence is everywhere.

Bandcamp built a business on fans paying for music they could stream for free — and routinely paying *above* the asking price. Wikipedia runs on donations from people who could free-ride forever; US public radio now does too, entirely, since Congress eliminated its federal funding in 2025. The busker's hat fills even though the music stops for no one. Signed copies sell at a premium to people who already own the book.

People don't pay for the copy. They pay the person — for provenance (*this came from you*) and for continuation (*make more of it*). The creative economy already runs on this impulse; copyright was an inefficient tax layered on top, collected mostly by intermediaries. But the impulse has one hard requirement: you have to be able to find the person.

## Laws that point

That is the real problem of a world without copyright — not how to stop copies, but how to keep the source visible when copying is free. It's a much smaller problem, and it wants a much smaller law.

Not exclusion: attribution. A right to be credited, which already exists in much of the world as [moral rights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moral_rights), held separately from the economic ones. Fraud law against anyone who passes your work off as theirs, which exists everywhere. And technology doing the heavy lifting: signed releases, canonical sources, provenance standards like [C2PA](https://c2pa.org/). Laws and norms that point to originals, instead of fences around copies. The fence never worked. The pointer is all we ever needed.

This essay should practice what it preaches, so here is its provenance. I argued these ideas out in a long dialogue with one language model, and another helped me put them into words. They read more of the web than I ever will. The argument is mine because I chose it, pushed back on it, cut it, and stand behind it. No law required me to tell you any of that — and that's the scaffolding working. The pointing is the point.

## The last scarcity

Which is why I don't think authenticity drowns in the flood. It's the one thing the flood can't produce. A model can imitate a voice; it cannot have a life. It can't mean something at its own expense, can't stand behind a claim, can't be found and thanked. As the generated everything rises, it strips away the formulaic filler that was already half-machine — and what remains visible is what was always valuable: a real person, with a real history, who made this.

Copyright spent three centuries answering *who may copy this?* It was never the question that mattered.

Who made this? I did, with machines. Now you know where to point.
